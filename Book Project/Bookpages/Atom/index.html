Description:

"Atom" is a history of man's attempt over 2500 years to answer the question of how finely can matter be divided.  You will be guided from the first idea of an "atom" to physicist's present understanding of the building blocks of the universe, in a non-technical manner.
Recommended for:  students who are interested in the history of modern physics.  There is a lot of information, but there is a flowing narrative.

Did-you-read questions:

Deadline:   February 1, 2005.
These should be easy questions, but you should have the questions ready and keep an eye out during your reading. Each question should be answered with 1-2 sentences.
Note: these questions are not probably not the most important part of the book! Your essay will not have to cover these questions!
  1. For what idea is James Maxwell most famous?
  2. What is the true identity of "cathode-ray particles"?
  3. Why is "packing fraction" important in determining which nuclei are stable?
  4. What was the problem with beta-emission, and how was it resolved?
  5. How many quarks do we believe exist, and what are they?
Report Questions:

Deadline: Peer Review Session on February 8, 2005; paper due February 10, 2005.
You should write a 3 - 4 page essay on one of the following questions. Your essay should include examples and references to the book, unless otherwise specified. Page number references are sufficient for citing material from the primary book. If you use outside materials, cite your sources in full. If you would rather write on a different topic, you may, but clear it with Mr. Howe or Ms. Sullivan first.

  1. This book was published in 1992, and several possibilities that Asimov mentioned have been resolved. For example: the top quark has been detected and its mass measured, and there is at least some evidence that neutrinos have mass. Do some research on one or more of these topics, and explain how it extends and/or contradicts ideas in the book.
  2. Asimov finishes the text with "It shows what asking the right questions can bring about" How do people find these questions? Can you think of others that could keep people occupied for over 2500 years?
  3. Do you think that Einstein's (and many other's) quest for a grand unified theory is possible? Is it relevant to humanity?
  4. Asimov presented many ideas of various scientists that turned out later to be wrong. This is the process of science, and it explains why progress is sometimes slow. Pick one of these wrong ideas, and try to imagine the present world if this theory had not been replaced.
