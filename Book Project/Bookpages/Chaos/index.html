Description:

How do some calculations depend very sensitively on the initial conditions of a problem?  One common example of this is the butterfly effect:  the flap of a butterfly's wings over India may result in a hurricane in the Gulf of Mexico.  Related to this is the idea of self-symmetry:  if you look at a small portion of a large object, it may resemble or duplicate the large object. A look of the elegance and simplicity that arise when things get too complicated for today's math and science.
Recommended for: Math enthusiasts, computer scientists, and people that think that science and math are too idealized and aren't "messy" enough.

Note: for your graphical presentation, you may choose to write a computer program to calculate and draw one or more of the chaotic structures in this book. See Mr. Howe for details.

  2. How long is the coastline of Britain?
  3. What is the Mandelbrot set?
  4. What was the power of Huberman's model of erratic eye movement?
  5. Give an example of an object that displays self-similarity.
Report Questions:

Deadline: Peer Review Session on February 8, 2005; paper due February 10, 2005.
You should write a 3 - 4 page essay on one of the following questions. Your essay should include examples and references to the book, unless otherwise specified. Page number references are sufficient for citing material from the primary book. If you use outside materials, cite your sources in full. If you would rather write on a different topic, you may, but clear it with Mr. Howe or Ms. Sullivan first.

  1. Is the study of chaos worthy of being called "science"? Give arguments for both sides.
  2. Some of the most complex results come from very simple equations.  Choose a simple event in people's lives, and discuss how so many complex outcomes can result.
  3. Did Chaos give you a hopeful outlook on some of the technological problems we face, such a weather predictions and noise reductions, or do you feel that these problems can never be completely solved?
  4. In many ways, this is a math text with applications across most disciplines of science.  Pick a specific physics application, and discuss why a knowledge of chaos theory is critical to its understanding.
